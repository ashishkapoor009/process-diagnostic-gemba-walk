# Lean Six Sigma

## Overview
Lean Six Sigma combines Lean's focus on eliminating waste and improving flow
with Six Sigma's focus on reducing variation and defects using statistical
rigor (DMAIC: Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control). A Master Black
Belt applies both lenses simultaneously: first make the process flow (Lean),
then make it predictable and defect-free (Six Sigma).

## The Eight Wastes (TIMWOODS)
- **Transportation** - unnecessary movement of data, documents, or materials between systems, people, or locations.
- **Inventory** - work sitting idle: unprocessed tickets, queued approvals, backlogs.
- **Motion** - unnecessary physical or digital movement (switching between 6 systems to complete one task).
- **Waiting** - idle time between steps: waiting for approval, waiting for a system response, waiting for another team.
- **Overprocessing** - doing more work than the customer/downstream step requires (excessive checks, redundant approvals, over-formatted reports).
- **Overproduction** - producing output before it's needed, or more than needed (generating reports nobody reads).
- **Defects** - errors requiring rework, correction, or scrap.
- **Skills/Unused Talent** - not using people's full capability, or assigning skilled staff to low-value manual work.

## Value-Added vs Non-Value-Added Analysis
Every step should be classified as:
- **VA (Value Added)** - the customer would pay for it; it transforms the product/service.
- **NVA (Non-Value Added)** - pure waste; should be eliminated (e.g. re-keying data already captured upstream).
- **BNVA (Business Non-Value-Added)** - required for compliance, risk, or business reasons but doesn't directly add customer value (e.g. mandatory audit logging, regulatory approval). Target for simplification, not elimination.

Typical mature operations achieve 30-50% VA ratio; unoptimized processes are
often below 20% VA, with the remainder split between necessary BNVA
(approvals, compliance checks) and pure NVA waste (waiting, rework, hand-offs).

## DMAIC for Process Improvement
1. **Define** - process boundaries, customer requirements (CTQs), problem statement.
2. **Measure** - baseline cycle time, touch time, wait time, defect rate, FTE, volume, AHT.
3. **Analyze** - root cause analysis (5 Whys, Fishbone/Ishikawa, Pareto) to separate symptoms from root causes.
4. **Improve** - design and pilot the future-state process; quantify savings (FTE, cost, cycle time, AHT).
5. **Control** - SOPs, control charts, dashboards, and governance to sustain the gain.

## Root Cause Analysis Techniques
- **5 Whys** - iteratively ask "why" to move from symptom to root cause.
- **Fishbone (Ishikawa)** - categorize causes into People, Process, Technology, Policy, Environment, Materials.
- **Pareto Analysis** - 80/20 rule; prioritize the ~20% of causes driving ~80% of the defects/delay.

## Hand-offs and Bottlenecks
Every hand-off between people, teams, or systems introduces wait time, risk
of information loss, and rework potential. Processes with more than 3-4
hand-offs per transaction are strong standardization/automation candidates.
Bottlenecks are the slowest step(s) constraining total throughput (Theory of
Constraints) - fixing a non-bottleneck step rarely improves end-to-end cycle time.

## Typical Efficiency Gains
Well-executed Lean Six Sigma + automation initiatives on manual, hand-off-heavy
back-office processes typically yield 20-40% reduction in cycle time and AHT,
and 15-30% FTE capacity release, once quick wins, standardization, and
automation are combined - consistent with the 25-30% target efficiency
improvement used as this tool's benchmark.
